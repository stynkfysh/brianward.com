Understanding Retrospective Appraisals

A date of death appraisal is a unique type of appraisal where the effective date is in the past. Unlike standard appraisals that value a property as of today, retrospective appraisals determine what a property was worth on a specific date that may have been anywhere from one month to more than 30 years ago.

These appraisals are essential for estate settlement, establishing tax bases, and other legal purposes. They require specialized knowledge of historical market data and trends to accurately determine fair market value on that historical date.

Distinction from Standard Appraisals

Standard mortgage appraisals determine current property value and are bound by lending guidelines. Date of death appraisals are different:

  • Effective date is in the past, not the current date
  • No lender guidelines or restrictions
  • Focus on historical comparable properties from the effective date
  • Require interpretation of historical market data and trends
  • Used for legal, tax, and estate purposes
  • Standard appraisals cannot be used as date of death appraisals

Common Uses for Date of Death Appraisals

Estate Settlement

Determining property value for estate distribution among heirs and calculating taxes owed to the estate.

Tax Base Establishment

Setting the "stepped-up basis" for inherited property, which has significant tax implications for heirs.

Probate Proceedings

Providing documented appraisal evidence for court proceedings related to estate administration.

Insurance and Legal Claims

Establishing property value on a specific historical date for various legal and financial purposes.

Our Expertise in Historical Market Data

Determining the value of a property on a date in the past requires more than simply looking back at old listing prices. Our appraisers have the expertise to:

  • Research historical market conditions and trends
  • Locate comparable sales from the effective date period
  • Adjust for market fluctuations and economic conditions
  • Document and justify all methodology and assumptions
  • Provide reports suitable for legal and tax purposes

Important Note About Desktop Appraisals

Desktop appraisals (estimates based on publicly available data without property inspection) generally do not satisfy IRS requirements for date of death appraisals, with the exception of vacant land. For most properties, a Consumer Appraisal is the most commonly ordered option for date of death valuations.

Our Consumer Appraisals provide the thorough documentation and credibility that legal and tax authorities require.

Frequently Asked Questions

How far back can you appraise a property? +

We can appraise properties for dates ranging from recent (within the last month) to 30 or more years in the past. The feasibility depends on the availability of historical data for the specific property and time period. For very old effective dates, gathering sufficient comparable sales data may require additional research and time.

What documents will I need? +

We'll need documentation of the effective appraisal date, details about the property, and any relevant information about the estate or tax situation. For properties appraised many years in the past, we may request historical property records or information about any improvements made since the effective date.

How long does a date of death appraisal take? +

Consumer Appraisals typically take 5-7 business days. Historical appraisals may take longer if extensive research is required to locate appropriate comparable properties from the effective date. We'll provide an estimate based on the specific requirements of your appraisal.

Will this appraisal be accepted by the IRS? +

Our appraisals follow IRS guidelines and are prepared in a format acceptable for tax purposes. We use proper appraisal methodology, appropriate comparable properties, and provide thorough documentation. However, we recommend consulting with your tax advisor or estate attorney regarding specific IRS requirements for your situation.

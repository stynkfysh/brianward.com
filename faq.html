Frequently Asked Questions

What is a real estate appraisal?

An appraisal is an independent, professional estimate of what a home is worth. A certified appraiser reviews the property, compares it to similar homes that have recently sold nearby, and considers current market conditions to arrive at a fair market value. The result is a written report you can use for legal, tax, or personal purposes.

What types of appraisals do you offer?

We offer several options depending on your needs. A Desktop appraisal is done without visiting the property — the appraiser works from public records, photos, and market data. A Drive-By appraisal includes photographing the exterior without entering the home. A Standard appraisal is a full interior and exterior inspection. We also handle multi-unit properties (2–4 units). Not sure which you need? Just ask — we'll help you choose the right one. See our Appraisal Services & Fees page for details.

How much does an appraisal cost?

Fees start at $299 for a Basic Desktop Appraisal and go up to $725 for a Standard 2–4 Unit Appraisal. A Standard single-family appraisal is $625. Fees can vary for complex properties, large acreage, or rush delivery. Visit our Appraisal Services & Fees page for full pricing.

How long does it take to get my report?

Most reports are delivered within 3–5 business days after the inspection, or after you place the order for desktop appraisals. If you're on a tight deadline — for a court date, closing, or filing — we offer rush service. Just let us know when you reach out.

What is a date-of-death appraisal and when do I need one?

A date-of-death appraisal determines what a home was worth on the date someone passed away. It's used to establish the property's tax basis for the estate, which affects how much capital gains tax the heirs may owe if and when they sell. The IRS typically requires this type of appraisal during estate settlement. We can do these going back 30 or more years, depending on available market data. Learn more on our Date of Death Appraisal page.

I'm going through a divorce. How does the appraisal process work?

In a divorce, you need an independent value for the home so the property can be divided fairly. We provide a neutral appraisal that both parties can rely on, and we offer a two-client option so both spouses receive the report at a lower combined cost than ordering separately. If your case goes to court, we also provide expert witness testimony. We've completed over 1,000 divorce-related appraisals. Visit our Divorce Appraisal page for more details.

What is an estate or trust appraisal?

When someone passes away or a trust is being administered, the real estate usually needs to be valued for probate, tax reporting, or distribution to beneficiaries. An estate appraisal provides that official value. It can be done as of today's date or retroactively as of the date of death. Our reports meet court and IRS requirements. See our Estate Appraisal page to learn more.

Can I get an appraisal before I buy or sell a home?

Absolutely. A pre-purchase appraisal gives you an independent value before you make an offer, so you know whether the asking price is fair. A pre-sale appraisal helps you set a realistic listing price — it's more thorough than a real estate agent's market analysis and can prevent costly pricing mistakes. Either way, it's a relatively small investment that can save you thousands.

Do I need an appraisal for a bankruptcy filing?

Yes. If you own real estate and are filing Chapter 7, 11, or 13 bankruptcy, the court requires a current market value for your property. Our appraisals are court-compliant and meet the documentation standards that bankruptcy trustees and judges expect. Visit our Bankruptcy Appraisal page for more information.

Can you help with property tax appeals?

Yes. If you believe your county-assessed value is too high, an independent appraisal can support your appeal. We also prepare appraisals for gift tax purposes and stepped-up basis calculations for inherited property. All of our tax-related appraisals are documented to IRS standards. See our Tax Appraisal page for details.

What is a family transaction appraisal?

When you're buying or selling property to a family member, the IRS expects the transaction to be at fair market value. An independent appraisal protects everyone involved by documenting that the price is legitimate, which helps avoid tax penalties or gift tax issues down the road. Learn more on our Family Transaction Appraisal page.

Can an appraisal help me get rid of PMI?

Yes. Private mortgage insurance (PMI) is required when you have less than 20% equity in your home. If your home's value has gone up since you bought it, a new appraisal can prove you've reached that 20% threshold — and your lender may remove the PMI, potentially saving you hundreds of dollars per month. Visit our PMI Removal page to learn how.

Are your appraisals accepted by courts and the IRS?

Yes. All of our appraisals comply with USPAP (Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice), which is the standard recognized by courts, the IRS, and financial institutions. We have over 22 years of experience preparing reports for legal and tax proceedings.

Do I need to be home during the inspection?

You don't have to be, but you're welcome to be present. The appraiser will photograph the home and note its condition, features, and any upgrades. If you're there, you can point out improvements that might not be obvious — like a new roof, updated plumbing, or a remodeled kitchen.

What do you need from me to get started?

Just the property address, what the appraisal is for (divorce, estate, buying/selling, etc.), and a specific date if you need a retrospective value. You can call us at (888) 844-1733, email contact@brianward.com, or fill out our online form — we'll take it from there.

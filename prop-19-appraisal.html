Proposition 19 Appraisal Service

California's Proposition 19, which took effect in February 2021, fundamentally changed how inherited properties are treated for property tax purposes. The law significantly narrowed the protections previously available under Proposition 58, which had allowed properties to pass from parent to child with minimal or no increase in assessed value for decades. Under Prop 19, most inherited properties now face reassessment at fair market value, triggering substantial increases in property tax liability. An accurate appraisal is critical to understanding the true financial impact of inheritance.

How Proposition 19 Changed Property Tax Treatment

Prior to February 16, 2021, California's Proposition 58 provided nearly unlimited protection for properties transferred from parent to child. The assessed value could remain at the base year value indefinitely, even if the property's market value increased dramatically. This exemption applied to all property types and had no value cap.

Proposition 19 substantially limited this benefit. Today, the parent-to-child transfer exemption applies only to the primary residence of the heir, and only if the heir actually occupies the property within one year of taking title. Additionally, the exemption includes a $1 million cap on the value above the base year value. For non-primary residences, investment properties, and properties where the value exceeds the base year plus $1 million, reassessment at fair market value is mandatory. This creates a clear need for accurate appraisals to determine the exact tax liability impact.

The Critical Role of Fair Market Value Appraisals

Under Proposition 19, the reassessment calculation is straightforward but heavily dependent on an accurate fair market value determination. The formula is: New Assessed Value = Base Year Value + Maximum(0, FMV − (Base Year Value + $1,000,000)). Every dollar of fair market value above the base year value plus the exemption threshold becomes taxable assessed value. An underestimation of fair market value results in underpayment of taxes and potential audit exposure. An overestimation creates unnecessary financial burden. We apply rigorous appraisal standards to ensure fair market value is accurately determined, giving our clients and their professionals confidence in the valuation used for tax planning and compliance.

Who Needs Proposition 19 Appraisals

Estate planning attorneys rely on Proposition 19 appraisals to advise clients about inheritance strategies and the true cost of acquiring property through inheritance. CPAs and tax professionals need these appraisals to prepare accurate property tax returns and advise clients on the impact of Prop 19. Trustees and executors managing estates need valuations to properly distribute assets and communicate the tax implications to heirs. Heirs themselves benefit from understanding the assessed value that will result from their inheritance, allowing them to make informed decisions about keeping or disposing of inherited property. Financial planners use these appraisals to model the long-term costs of property ownership for inherited real estate.

How Brian Ward Appraisal Handles Proposition 19 Cases

Most Proposition 19 appraisals involve two key elements. First, we provide or verify the base year value through historical appraisals performed at or near the date of death. We have extensive experience with retrospective appraisals dating from one month to over 30 years prior, so we can accurately establish what the property was worth when the decedent passed away. Second, we provide a current-date appraisal reflecting today's fair market value, which is then plugged into the Prop 19 reassessment formula. Our appraisers are deeply familiar with San Diego County market conditions and understand the historical context necessary to support both the historical and current valuations.

Connection to Date of Death Appraisals

Proposition 19 appraisals are closely related to the date of death appraisals for which we have built our reputation. Many inherited property situations require both services. A date of death appraisal establishes the base year value for Prop 19 purposes, while a current appraisal shows fair market value. Together, these two valuations allow the Prop 19 formula to be accurately applied. If you are managing an estate that includes inherited property, we can coordinate both services to streamline the process and ensure consistent, professional valuation work.
